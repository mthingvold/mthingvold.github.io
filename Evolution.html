Evolution - Change in the heritable characteristics of living organisms over successive generation

  • descent with modification through earths history
  • in terms of genetics, is "change of gene frequency through time"

Two competing viewpoints on life:

  • Organisms are fixed, and do not change over time
  • Organisms change over time

Traditionally, most people believed that life was fixed or static - never changing

  • Ex. The plenum or fullness of the world
  • Evidence of extinction contradicted this

The discovery of fossils throughout the 19th century showed that strange creature once existed that are no longer alive today

  • Transmutation - an early 'pre-evolutionary' model that stated there were direct ancestor-descendent relationships between species

  • alfred russel wallace

Variability - there is variation in all populations of organisms

  • No two members of pop are identical
  • Age sex, inherited traits, lifestyle etc.
  • The idea that individuals are variable was novel, as early naturalists believed in 'perfect types'

Heritability - Variation is inherited or at least some discovery of genes -> discovery of dna heritable traits are coded in DNA with genes carrying different alleles

  • dominant Recessive

Different combinations of alleles result in different genotypes, and the physical expression of these genotypes results in a phenotype

Mutations - new variations in inheritable traits caused by miscopied DNA

  • Some are harmful, some neutral, and others are beneficial

Superfecundity - Organisms produce more offspring than can survive reproductive excess

Natural selection is the mechanism by which organisms change analogous to artificial selection selection/domestication

Natural selection was defined by:

  • Variability

    Species have micro and macro evolutionary changes demonstrated through concepts of: divergence Common Ancestry

    Common ancestry A group of organisms shares a common ancestor

    • Closely related species are close because their common ancestor diverged relatively recently in earths history
    • Other species are more distant because their ancestors diverge at a further (back) point in time
    • Complex patterns of common ancestry and descendants

    Divergent evolution - the accumulation of difference between groups, leading to the formation of a new species

    • Two (or more) distinct variations in an ancestral population have their own advantages in geologic time
    • over time, these differences will become more pronounced
    • If they diverge enough, they will no longer be able to mate with one another

    Taxonomy - A named group of organisms

    • Traditionally, each culture had its own name for the animals, plants and other organisms in their own region
    • linnaeus made universal set ofrules in 1758 to rank organisms
    • standardized organism naming and created a framework to compare relatedness
    • Linnean Rules:
      • All names in latin/greek, modified to fit in latin form
      • Each name unique
      • nested hierarchy(species,genera,family,order,etc)
      • traditional official ranks:
        • primary unit is species
        • net unit is genus which is >1 species
    • Species - Naturally ocurring populations that interbreed and produce viable offspring
    • Problem in fossil record - cannot test interbreeding/behaviors
    • The question therefore becomes, how different do two individuals or populations have to be for them to be different species?

    Systematics and phylogeny

    • Systematics - A method of organizing organisms
      • different from taxonomy, in that it seek to determine how organisms are related to one another
    • Phylogeny - the evolutionary history of an organism
      • reflection of patterns of ancestors and descendants, as all living things come from a common origin, therefore possible to reconstruct
      • From this cladistics was born

    Cladistics - a method of classification of animals and plants according to the proportion of measurable characteristics that they have in common

    • it was recognized that finding fossils which are direct ancestor of any species would be difficult
    • Instead, the focus shifted towards the distribution of derived characters (evolutionary specializations)
    • In cladistics not all traits are considered useful in figuring out evolutionary relationships
      • Primitive (ancestral) traits do not help
      • Derived (unique) character states are how relatedness is determined
      • Convergent characters can be misleading
        • as are reversal states
      • Shared characterists is how we construct patterns of common ancestry
