Igneous Rocks Classified by composition and grain size

  • composition:
    • Felsic: granite, rhyolite
    • Mafic: BASALT, Gabbro
    • Grain Size:
      • A direct reflection of the rate at which the rock cooled (Rapid: Fine grained extrusive, Slow: large grained intrusive

Intrusive igneous rocks cool slowly and form large visible crystals --cool from magma. Extrusive igneous rocks cool qucikly and form microscopic crystals. --cools from lava

Chemical comp can also indicate the source: If rocks are from magma derived from the mantle they are called mafic. If rocks undergo considerable mixing with continental rock, it will be felsic low in Mg and Fe and high in Si and Al

Metamorphic Rocks

  • Form by alteration of other rocks at temperatures and pressures greater than at the Earth's surface
    • Rocks change chemical composition without melting
    • Form from a parent rock which could be igneous/sedimentary/metamorphic The degree of change in the parent rock depends on the type and intesnity of metamorphic processes involved:
    • heat
    • Pressure
    • Chemical fluids

Regional metamorphism

  • Responsible for most metamorphic rocks, and is the result of mountain building at convergent boundaries
  • Grade * Leavel of temperature and pressure of metamorphism
  • Foliation * Alignment of platy minerals caused by applied pressures

With increased temperature and pressure, there will be increasing metamorphic grade and foliation

  • foliaton only occurs when specific platy minerals make up the rock composition *
  • Marble: Calcite/dolomite with limestone parent
  • Quartzite: Almost pure quartz with sandstone parent

Contact metamorphism

  • igneous intrusion "bakes" surrounding rocks

Hydrothermal metamorphism

  • Results from percolation of hot watery fluids

Burial Metamorphism

  • Similar to regional

Sedimentary rocks form from the accumulation and lithification of bits of previously existing rock and/or organic matter.

Sedimentary

Bits of previously existing rock/organic matter is called sediment because sedimentary rocks form by deposition they create horizontal beds called strata Major divisions of sedimentary rocks reflects the type of sediment:

  • Siliciclastic/Detrital
  • Biological
  • Chemical

Sediments produced by:

  • Weathering, erosion of other rocks
  • Crystals precipitated from seawater
  • Skeletal debris from ogranisms

Siliciclastic/detrital rocks -Sed rocks composed of clasts of silicate minerals Cemented together with minerals from groundwater after they are buried

Basic siliciclastic cycle:

  1. Uplift, causing erosion
  2. Transport through wind and/or water to a centre of deposition
  3. Burial, resulting in lithification
    • often due to cementation via dissolved minerals in groundwater

As distance from source rock increases, so does degree of:

  • Roudning
  • Sorting
  • Maturity

Classification based on grain size and shape:

  • Gravel:
    • >2mm diameter
    • Granules, pebbles, cobbles, boulders
  • Conglomerate:
    • Rounded grains
  • Breccia:
    • Angular grain
  • Sand:
    • 1/16 - 2mm diameter
    • often quartz
    • sandstone
  • Silt:
    • 1/256-1/16mm
  • Clay/Mud:
    • Smaller than 1/256 mm
  • Mudstones:
    • Shale
      • Fissile mudstone

Evaporties

readily formed, readily dissolved

Chert

  • extremely small quartz crystals precipitated from watery solutions
  • Brown, gray, or black impurities
  • Flint

Any Sedimentary rock that is made from biological material is biological in nature: Coal and limestones

Carbonates: Limestone:

  • Often formed from skeletal particles of organisms that secrete calcium carbonate
  • Typically forms in warm, shallow sea water
  • Dissolves relatively easily in water, and in acid
  • Commonly deposited as:
    • Fossil reef (fossilifeous limestone)
    • Shelled mollusk accumulation (bioclastic limestone)
    • Accumulation of microscopic organsims with calcareous shells (chalks)

  • Reflect modes of deposition
  • Useful tools for interpreting ancient environment
