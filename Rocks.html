Igneous Rocks Classified by composition and grain size

  • composition:
    • Felsic: granite, rhyolite
    • Mafic: BASALT, Gabbro
    • Grain Size:
      • A direct reflection of the rate at which the rock cooled (Rapid: Fine grained extrusive, Slow: large grained intrusive

Intrusive igneous rocks cool slowly and form large visible crystals --cool from magma. Extrusive igneous rocks cool qucikly and form microscopic crystals. --cools from lava

Chemical comp can also indicate the source: If rocks are from magma derived from the mantle they are called mafic. If rocks undergo considerable mixing with continental rock, it will be felsic low in Mg and Fe and high in Si and Al

Metamorphic Rocks

  • Form by alteration of other rocks at temperatures and pressures greater than at the Earth's surface
    • Rocks change chemical composition without melting
    • Form from a parent rock which could be igneous/sedimentary/metamorphic The degree of change in the parent rock depends on the type and intesnity of metamorphic processes involved:
    • heat
    • Pressure
    • Chemical fluids

Regional metamorphism

  • Responsible for most metamorphic rocks, and is the result of mountain building at convergent boundaries
  • Grade * Leavel of temperature and pressure of metamorphism
  • Foliation * Alignment of platy minerals caused by applied pressures

With increased temperature and pressure, there will be increasing metamorphic grade and foliation

  • foliaton only occurs when specific platy minerals make up the rock composition *
  • Marble: Calcite/dolomite with limestone parent
  • Quartzite: Almost pure quartz with sandstone parent

Contact metamorphism

  • igneous intrusion "bakes" surrounding rocks

Hydrothermal metamorphism

  • Results from percolation of hot watery fluids

Burial Metamorphism

  • Similar to regional

Sedimentary rocks form from the accumulation and lithification of bits of previously existing rock and/or organic matter.

Sedimentary

Bits of previously existing rock/organic matter is called sediment because sedimentary rocks form by deposition they create horizontal beds called strata Major divisions of sedimentary rocks reflects the type of sediment:

  • Siliciclastic/Detrital
  • Biological
  • Chemical

Sediments produced by:

  • Weathering, erosion of other rocks
  • Crystals precipitated from seawater
  • Skeletal debris from ogranisms

Siliciclastic/detrital rocks -Sed rocks composed of clasts of silicate minerals Cemented together with minerals from groundwater after they are buried

Basic siliciclastic cycle:

  1. Uplift, causing erosion
  2. Transport through wind and/or water to a centre of deposition
  3. Burial, resulting in lithification
    • often due to cementation via dissolved minerals in groundwater

As distance from source rock increases, so does degree of:

  • Roudning
  • Sorting
  • Maturity

Any Sedimentary rock that is made from biological material is biological in nature: Coal and limestones

Carbonates: Limestone:

Facies:

  • Set of characteristics, determined by sedimentary structures, lithological features, and fossils that define a particular sed environment.
  • Facies are small scale, where sedimentary environments are concerned with large scale patterns
  • This is represented by Walther's Law:
    • As depositional environments migrate laterally the sediments
    • of one environment come to lie on top of sediments of the adjacent environment

Sedimentary Environments

There are three main categories sedimentary environments can be grouped into:

  1. Terrestrial
  2. Marginal Marine
  3. Marine

Terrestrial

Paleosol:

  • Fossil soil
  • Formed from erosion and weathering of host rock, and have an organic component due to plant growth.
  • Root traces and animal burrows are an important indicator
  • Associated with coal

Aluvial Fans:

  • Low, cone-shaped structures
  • Develop where mountain slope meets valley floor
  • Change in slope reduces stream velocity (often form breccias)

Transport of Sediment by Streams:

  • Water and sediment move from mountains to sea through a variety of depositional systems
  • Total load = all stream transported material
    • Dissolved load - ion rich water from groundwater
    • Suspended load - controlled by water velocity and grain size
    • Bed load - particles move by rolling, sliding, saltation

Types of stream systems - Braided Streams:

  • Complex Network of flow paths Dominated by coarse sediment - Meandering streams:
  • Channel on low gradient floodplain in shape of sweeping bends, stream erodes at cut banks, deposits at point bars - Anastomosed streams:
  • True, deep interwoven channels dominated by fine sediment

Braided systems are generally straight channels in areas of high relief they store coarse grained materials like sand and gravel Channels tend to be wide and relatively shallow.

Meandering Systems Are winding, bending channels in plain areas They host fine grained material (silt and sand) that are carried in suspension There is high channel stability and the rivers tend to be deep and narrow Channels, point bars, levees, floodplains

Anastomosing systems Winding, with multiple interconnected channels in low gradient areas (Think of a branching graph or a trees roots) Stable, deep channels Fine graining (silt/mud)

Terrestrial Environments

  • Lacustrine (Lakes) - an area filled with water in a localized basin that is surrounded by land
    • Lower elevation, more likely preservation
  • Sediments:
    • Coarsest around lake margin
    • Finest at center (often layered
  • Freshwater fossils

Lakes

  • Open lakes:

    • freshwater
  • Closed Lakes:
    • Without an outlet
    • Fluctuating shorelines
